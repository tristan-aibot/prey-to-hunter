Chapter 09 — Life Skills · Article 01

Managing time and energy

Being busy and being productive are two completely different things. Most people are busy. The hunter is productive — and knows how to protect the rest of his energy.

Time is finite. Energy is rechargeable.

Classic productivity advice revolves around time: plan your calendar, block hours, use the Pomodoro technique. But time isn't the real problem. Everyone has 24 hours. The problem is energy.

You can have a full calendar and still get nothing done — if your energy level is at zero. And you can have three hours and move mountains — if you're focused and rested. Protect your energy, not just your time.

The MIT method: Most Important Tasks

Every morning, before you open your email or check your phone: write down two or three MITs. Not a to-do list of twenty items — two or three things that, if done, count the day as a success.

Do those things first, when your energy is highest. Everything after is a bonus. Most people do the opposite: they start with small, easy tasks to feel like they're being productive — and at the end of the day the heavy work still hasn't been done.

"You don't need to do more. You need to stop doing things that don't matter."

The art of saying no

Every time you say yes to something, you're saying no to something else. A one-hour meeting isn't just one hour lost — it's also the focus you lose before and after from switching context.

Saying no isn't unfriendliness. It's clarity about where your energy goes. A structured refusal respects both your time and theirs more than a half-hearted yes.

Managing energy: the four sources

Physical: sleep, exercise, nutrition. Without this foundation, nothing works. You can't deliver cognitive peak performance if your body is running on reserves.

Mental: protecting focus time, actively removing distraction. Notifications off. Phone out of sight. Single-tasking instead of multitasking.

Emotional: not walking around with unresolved conflicts, worries or anxieties that create background noise. This costs more energy than most people realize.

Purpose: knowing why you do what you do. Work that feels meaningful costs less energy than work that feels empty, even if the task is identical.

Recovery isn't a reward. It's a requirement.

Top athletes train hard AND recover systematically. They sleep enough. They take rest days. Not because they're lazy — but because they understand that recovery is part of the performance.

The same applies to mental work. A brain that never rests performs structurally worse. Schedule recovery in your week the way you schedule work. An evening doing nothing isn't wasted time — it's investing in tomorrow.
