Training for beginners

You don't need to become an athlete. You just need to be better than yesterday — and that starts with the first step.

Cardio vs strength

Cardio is good for your heart, brain, mood and endurance. Strength training is good for your metabolism, posture, bone density, hormonal balance and confidence. As a beginner: do both. They complement each other.

But if you have to choose one, choose strength. The benefits stack more broadly across more areas of your life. And muscle is metabolically active — it helps you even outside the gym.

Progressive overload

This is the only principle you truly need. Progressive overload means: do a little more than last week. One extra rep. Two more kilos. One second longer. The body adapts to the demands placed on it.

Without progressive overload you maintain yourself — you build nothing. This is why people train for years without results: they do the same thing every time. The body has no reason to adapt.

"Take care of your body. It's the only place you have to live." — Jim Rohn

Three exercises that cover everything

Squat, deadlift, and a horizontal push (push-up or bench press). These three compound movements train the vast majority of your muscles in one movement. As a beginner, you can extract months — or years — of progress from just these three.

Learn the technique properly before adding weight. One bad technique that becomes a habit costs far more to unlearn than learning it correctly from the start. Watch instruction videos. Seek feedback.

Recovery is the training

Muscles don't grow in the gym. They grow during recovery. The gym is the stimulus — sleep, nutrition and rest are the response. Training two to three times per week with good recovery is structurally more effective than training six times without rest.

More is not always better. A body that never rests makes no progress — it just survives. And surviving is not growing.

Consistency over perfection

A mediocre programme you follow for twelve months is better than a perfect programme you abandon after three weeks. Choose something achievable. Do it consistently. Then optimise.

The best training is the training you keep doing. Start smaller than you think you should. Build from there. Let it become part of your life — not a project.
