Public speaking

The fear of speaking in front of a group is greater than the fear of death — so the famous study says. But it is also one of the most trainable skills there is.

Normalising nerves

Nervousness before speaking is universal. Even the best speakers feel it. The difference: they use it as fuel instead of trying to suppress it.

Heart rate up, adrenaline: that is your body saying "this matters". Not: "this is going wrong." Relabel the nervousness as excitement — they feel physically identical, but the meaning is different. That relabelling literally changes how you perform.

Preparation is the only antidote

Most speaking anxiety comes from one source: the feeling that you don't know enough. Solution: know it well. Prepare until you can explain the material without notes.

Not memorising — understanding. A memorised text breaks the moment you forget one word. An understood story can always be retold, even if you lose the thread briefly. After that, you can improvise.

"It usually takes me more than three weeks to prepare a good impromptu speech." — Mark Twain

Structure: hook, core, close

Start with a hook: a question that resonates with the audience, a surprising statistic, a short story. The hook captures attention and holds it.

Then give your core point in maximum three sub-points. Three is the most people remember. End with a clear conclusion or call-to-action. Simple and powerful. Tell them what you're going to say, say it, then tell them what you said.

Pause as a weapon

The most underrated tool in public speaking. A two-second pause feels like an eternity to the speaker, but to the audience it feels like a moment of rest and authority.

Don't fill silence with "uh" or "so yeah" — use it. Pause after an important point. Pause before starting a new section. Pause says: I know what I'm doing. And the audience believes you.

Practice over talent

You don't become a better speaker by thinking about it. Only by doing it. Toastmasters, presentations at work, a YouTube channel, a speech for a small group of friends.

Every time you speak you get better. There is no shortcut. The people who seem "naturally" good at speaking have almost always simply put in more hours than everyone else.
