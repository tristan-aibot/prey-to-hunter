Chapter 06 — Money · Article 01

How money really works

In school you learn arithmetic. But nobody explains why one person builds wealth on the same salary another person can't stretch. That gap isn't luck — it's a knowledge gap.

Money is a system, not a number

Most people think of money as a number in an account. But money is a system: it flows in (income), it flows out (expenses), and it can work (investing) or sit still (savings at 0.01% interest).

The first thing to understand: money that stands still loses value. Inflation eats into your purchasing power year after year. Leaving money in a savings account is pretending nothing matters — when in reality you're getting slightly poorer each year relative to prices.

Income versus wealth

High income and wealth are not the same thing. Some people earn €8,000 a month and are always short. And some people earn €2,500 and are slowly building wealth.

The difference isn't the amount. It's the ratio between what comes in and what goes out — and what's left over from the difference to work for you.

Building wealth doesn't start with a high salary. It starts with creating margin: setting aside a portion of what you earn before you spend it.

"Rich people buy assets. The middle class buys liabilities hoping they're assets. Poor people only buy consumer goods." — Robert Kiyosaki

The three buckets

A simple framework that works: divide your monthly net income into three categories.

Living (60–70%): rent, groceries, transport, bills — things that actually need to happen. Not your lifestyle inflation, your real costs.

Buffer (10–15%): an emergency fund of three to six months of fixed costs. Not for nice things — for when your car breaks down, your job stops, or you get seriously ill. Without a buffer, emergencies force you into expensive solutions.

Investing (15–25%): everything that works for you while you sleep. Index ETFs, real estate, your own business — it doesn't matter, as long as it sets compound interest in motion.

Compound interest: the eighth wonder of the world

If you invest €200 per month at age 25 in a broadly diversified index with 7% average annual return, you'll have over €520,000 at 65. If you only start at 35, that drops to around €240,000. Ten years' difference — almost €280,000 less.

Compound interest doesn't work fast. But it works reliably. The only thing you need to do is start and not stop.

Making spending conscious

You don't need to become a miser. But you do need to become aware. Track where your money goes for one month — not to punish yourself, but to see where you're unconsciously leaking. Subscriptions you forget. Food you throw away. Impulse buys on Friday night.

Once you see where it goes, you can choose. And choosing is the core of everything that has to do with money.
