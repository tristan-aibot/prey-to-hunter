Selling without being pushy

Selling has a bad reputation. Deservedly, for those who do it wrong. But selling is essentially: helping — and getting paid for it.

Selling is problem-solving

The best salespeople aren't smooth talkers. They are people who listen first, then understand, then offer. If your offer genuinely matches the client's problem, it doesn't feel like selling. It feels like a logical next step.

The biggest sales problem most people have: they talk too much about their product and too little about the client's problem. Reverse that. You are not interesting. The problem you solve is interesting.

Truly understanding the problem

Before you offer anything: ask three questions. What is their problem? What have they already tried? What would the solution be worth to them? Those three answers determine whether your offer is relevant — and how to present it.

Most people skip this step. They go straight to the pitch. Then they don't understand why the client says no. The client says no because the salesperson didn't understand what the real problem was.

"People don't buy what you do; they buy why you do it." — Simon Sinek

Consultative selling

Position yourself as an advisor, not a salesperson. Give value before you ask for anything. Share knowledge. Ask good questions. If people already see you as an expert, the question isn't "do I want to buy something from you?" but "when can you start?"

This works best in B2B and higher-ticket sales. You build trust over multiple conversations. You share insights freely. And when the purchase decision moment comes, you're the one they trust — not the competitor who only pitched.

Objections are questions

"It's too expensive" means: I don't see the value yet. "I need to think about it" means: I need more information or I don't trust you enough yet. "Now isn't a good time" sometimes means: the problem isn't urgent enough.

Treat objections not as rejection but as requests for clarity. Answer the underlying question. Ask an open follow-up: "What would you need to see to know this is worth the investment?" That conversation delivers more than defending your price.

Follow-up without being annoying

Most deals die not from a no but from silence. One follow-up after a week: normal and expected. Two follow-ups over two weeks: still acceptable. After that: let it go — unless there's a concrete reason to stay in touch.

A good follow-up adds value: a relevant article, a market insight, an answer to a question they had. Not "have you thought about it?" That pushes. A good follow-up says: I'm thinking about your problem, even when you aren't.
