Freelancing as a first step

You don't need to build a company to work for yourself. Freelancing is the lowest barrier to financial independence — and for many, the best school.

Why freelancing works

Freelancing requires zero starting capital. You sell a skill you already have — or are developing. The margin is high: no office rent, no staff, no inventory. You learn directly how the market values your work.

And you build a portfolio of real projects. Not hypothetical cases, not a school assignment — real clients, real problems, real results. That's more valuable than any degree for your next step, whether that's a better job or your own business.

Turning your skills into a service

Writing, design, coding, translation, bookkeeping, marketing, video production, consulting. Every professional skill can be sold as a service. The question isn't: do I have a skill? The question is: who has a problem I can solve?

Start with what you're already good at — not with what you think the market wants. The market pays for quality and trust. Trust you build by delivering. Quality you already have, or you build it while you work.

"You don't need to be great to start, but you need to start to be great."

Finding your first client

Your network is bigger than you think. Start with people you know: colleagues, former classmates, family with a business. Tell them what you do. Be specific: not "I do marketing" but "I help small businesses find more clients through LinkedIn."

Offer the first job at a reduced rate in exchange for a testimonial and permission to include the work in your portfolio. The second client follows much more easily than the first. And the third. After three clients you have proof it works — and that proof is your strongest sales argument.

Setting your rates

Don't set your rate based on what you think you're worth — that's always too low. Set it based on two things: what does the market pay for comparable work, and what is the value for the client?

Hourly rate to start. Then move to project pricing — better for you (no cap on your income per hour) and for the client (no surprises). Raise your rate every time you're fully booked. If no one ever says "too expensive," you're too cheap.

From freelancer to entrepreneur

Freelancing is a job you do for yourself. Entrepreneurship is building a system that works without you. The bridge: productise your service. Fixed packages instead of custom work. Recurring clients instead of one-off projects. Standardised processes you can explain to someone else.

Once you know how to do it, you can teach others how it's done. Then you outsource the execution and focus on growth. That is the moment freelancer becomes entrepreneur.
