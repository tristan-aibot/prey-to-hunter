How to dress

Clothing is communication. You don't need a big budget — you need a system.

Fit is everything

Brand doesn't matter. Cut does. A cheap shirt that fits well looks better than an expensive one that doesn't. This is rule one of clothing: fit above everything.

Go to a tailor for basics. Have trousers taken in slightly, sleeves shortened — it costs little and the difference is enormous. Most people walk around for years in clothes that don't really fit. You notice it, even if you can't name it.

Neutral colours as a foundation

Black, white, grey, navy, beige. These colours combine with everything. Build your wardrobe on a neutral base and add colour as accent — a scarf, a sneaker, a shirt.

Everything always looks coordinated without having to think about it. No mornings standing in front of the wardrobe wondering. Everything goes with everything.

"Dress for the job you want, not the job you have."

The capsule wardrobe

Less is more. Twenty items that all combine are worth more than eighty that don't. A capsule wardrobe in practice: two good jeans, five basic t-shirts or polos, two shirts, one good jacket or coat, two pairs of shoes.

Start here. Buy less, buy better. One good shirt gets worn more than three poor ones — and you feel better in it too.

Shoes count double

People look at your shoes. Studies show shoes have a disproportionately large influence on the first impression people form of you. Dirty or cheap shoes undermine an otherwise good outfit.

Invest in one pair of good shoes rather than five cheap pairs. And keep them clean. A few minutes of polishing per week is all it takes.

Reading the occasion

The best outfit is the outfit that fits the context. Too casual at a formal event shows you weren't paying attention. Too formal at a casual one does too — it feels forced.

Read the room and dress one level above expectation. Not two — that's overdressed. One level: that's deliberate and respectful without being heavy-handed.
